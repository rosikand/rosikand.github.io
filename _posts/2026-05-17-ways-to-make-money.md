## Finance 


### Investment banks 

The poster child for high finance and the wannabe Patrick Batemen's of the world. The idea here is that large company A wants to buy large company B but this has lots of nuance to it. How do you set the price that is agreed upon by both? Who does the negotiation? How is the deal structured? All of this is served through a middle man: the investment bank (which employs the thousands of ivy-league new grads who rome Wall Street eating Cava six times a week for lunch, crunching numbers for their bosses (or clients as they say) until 2 AM, acting like they own the world). The caveat is that these mergers and acquisitions (M&A) are quite large. Like very large amounts of money being shuffled around (and perhaps this is why keeping the 22-year old analysts in the office until the wee hours of the morning is necessary... the room for error is little). And so these banks take a small percentage of the cut for helping facilitate the transcation. Investment banks also help take company's public via IPO's.  

The business of investment banking is heavily client facing and at a high level, essentially a sales role. They make money from their clients. They don't sell a product to them (like the tech bro's do in startup land). They sell a service. 


### Management consultants 

Not exactly finance but it fits the mold. 

These firms employ fresh new grads from the ivy league to go in and sit with a business and work with them to strategize and solve their problems. As a consequence, these business's pay a hefty fee to the likes of McKinsey and Bain. A common complaint is the lack of verifibiality in outcomes, for both the customer and the firm. Engagements often last just a few months and most recommendations are not actually seen through by the consultant. 

The typical archetype I've seen here is club president style persona's. I would argue though, these firms do serve as a good training ground for learning about enterprise businesses, which is experience you likely wouldn't get anywhere else at this level. 

The key players here are MBB: McKinsey, Bain, and BCG. There are some smaller firms like LEK and Oliver Wydman. Then there are the big 4 firms like EY and Deloitte, which is where the non-ivy league consultants go. 



### Hedge funds 

> These firms manage funds (yes, there is a difference). Each fund is supported by *limited partners*. Each firm employs *general partners* to manage the money. The goal is to generate a rate of return for the investors (the LP's) that they wouldn't otherwise be able to achieve themselves.   

Hedge funds invest this capital into public markets, like stocks. The goal is to not only "beat the market" (S&P 500-style index's) but also "hedge" against market downturn. In other words, investors pool their money into these funds to provide them with a stable rate of return, usually slightly above market average, but also to serve as a guarentee that they won't lose money. 

These firms deploy several types of strategies to accomplish this goal. Some do them all (multi-strat) and some focus on just one. Some firms are operate via "pods" where several key people operate the pool of funds (multi-manager) whereas others are run by a single individual setting the strategy (single-manager). The likes of Citadel and Millenium best represent the former. D1 and Pershing Square are good examples of the latter.

### Private Equity 

As with hedge funds (and the rest of alternative investing), the goal here is to employ *general partners* to manage *limited partners'* money, but instead of passively investing in public stocks, they use this capital to buy businesses and sell them at a higher price. 

In some ways, PE is like real estate but replace land with businesses. 

The idea is that they can buy a (private) business (via a procedure called a leveraged buy-out, or LBO), scale it in some capacity, and then sell it at a higher price later on, often to another PE firm. They claim that they obtain this higher premium via improving operations and scaling the business. But in reality, what happens is more *margin expansion*: *financial engineering*, via *LBO*'s, drive the return for the PE firms. Deals are often brought in by investment banks via auctions; though some firms source deals organically. And perhaps more importantly, these businesses move up the value chain from one firm to another. An Upper Mid Market firm might buy a business, perform some financial engineering and improve the operations, and then sell it to an Upper Market firm for double the price they bought it for. In some ways, PE is like sucking out all the juice thats possible from a business, keeping the extracted juice for themselves, and then passing it on to the next, bigger firm to extract it even more. 

The hack here is that they rarely buy the business outright with pure equity. Instead, they use *leverage*, loading up the business they are about to buy, with debt. And so these firms only end up paying a handful of the price for what they are buying. This makes the economics a lot easier to scale: they only need to put down a little bit of the sale price to acquire the business. Perhaps the biggest hack is that if the business goes under, these firms don't actually need to pay off the debt, since the debt is under the business, not the firm. So they have a hedge in this lens. When I first learned about the LBO, it truly did seem like an infinite money glitch cheatcode. In some ways, it really is. 

Despite this, there are several problems with the PE space in today's landscape: 

1. **Consolidation** and crowding of the space: too much capital chasing too few deals. Smaller firms die out and can't compete with bigger firms on price. 
2. **Cost of capital is too high**: PE saw its best returns in the ZIRP-era, when interest rates were zero. This made it easy for PE firms to borrow and to use leverage. But with the ZIRP-era long gone, the economics of the entire industry has changed, making it much harder to survive. 
3. Older **partners aren't giving up seats** to younger employees: some of the top PE firms in the world employ partners who are nearing their 70s and 80s. This is problematic because the 30 and 40 year olds aren't able to make partner since their aren't any seats open.  
4. **Operational improvements are controversial**: one of the primary methods PE firms use to improve the valuation of the business is to cut costs via firing half of the employees. This has obvious first order effects: ordinary people's lives are ruined, getting fired from their jobs for no apparent reason, leading to unemployment. But perhaps even more dangerous are the second order effects: some of these businesses that PE firms buy are businesses that people depend on for their needs. Like housing and healthcare. When employees are fired in large quantities, the operations of the business often fails to thrive, leading the end consumer at a deficit. This has caused PE to be viewed negatively among the general public. 


The main players in this space are called Mega Funds (MF). Behind them are Upper Market funds (UM). Then Upper Middle Market (UMM). Then Middle Market (MM), and so on. The difference in categorization is in relation to the deal size they do and the firms AUM. 

Some familiar names include Blackstone, KKR (arguably the inventor of private equity), Bain Capital, Vista Equity Partners, Thoma Bravo, Warbug Pincus, and Francisco Partners. 


Some firms focus on specific domains, like Vista Equity Partners, which specializes in tech PE. Other firms are more general.  

PE is viewed as the holy grail for high finance strivers. It's where the best Patrick Batemen's from the banking world end up. Still the same vibe, but more analytical and cerebral. The focus is on deals, not clients. Partners who can bring in deals make it rain for the firm, and are paid handsomely via carry on the deals.
